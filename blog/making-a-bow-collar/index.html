How to Add a Bow Collar to a Blouse

Category: Tutorials 01 February 21

Bow collars pop up in the first row of a fashion scene relatively often. They are loved & presented by luxury brands like Gucci & Saint Laurent.

I still remember the Gucci “Guilty” advertisement, which stole my heart forever & Saint Laurent 2020–2021 Autumn–Winter collection with its bow-tie blouses & power-jackets is in the top of my favourites so far.

The bow collar can look modest, but it also may be seductive revealing that tiny bit of a collarbone. Feminine & luxurious it brings 70-s vibes to one’s favourite blouse pattern. Cut it from the same fabric or not & make it narrow or extremely wide with the soft drapes. You are a designer here, after all. And this illustrated tutorial will accompany you on the way.

The Design

1. Decide on the design & proportions

Is the collar wide & long enough to make a double wrap & a puffy bow? Or, maybe it’s narrow & forms a small neat knot? Design options are up to you. For example, the ends of the collar can be wider than the middle part. In this case, you will need to cut two separate pieces for the collar & its inner facing.

More often the bow collar appears to be a single piece rectangle. In case if you need extra length for a dramatic effect, or you don’t have that much fabric left you can add a CB seam.

Also, don’t forget about the ends of the collar: they can be cut straight or diagonally. In this tutorial, I use the straight one-piece collar with square ends as an example.

2. Determine the dimensions

Usually, the width (W) of the finished collar is around 5–10 cm. But you are free to go beyond these numbers for a special effect.

As for the length (L), you can choose any number of centimetres or meters that you like. I can only note that for me it’s convenient to have a collar of at least 1.5 m to make a nice bow.

Below you can check pictures of my bow-collar blouses for length & width references.

Cutting

If your collar is based on the rectangular shape as mine, you don’t need to make a paper pattern, as you can draw directly on the fashion fabric.

You can cut it on the lengthwise or crosswise grain depending on the print, length of the collar & amount of fabric available. Sometimes this collar can even be cut on the bias for better drape.

To make sure the collar won’t become shorter & narrower when finished, don’t forget to add seam allowances to the length & width you have chosen. I would suggest adding 0.6–1.2 cm all around the collar piece. The best idea is to go with the seam allowance equal to the one of the neckline, as it makes sewing much easier.

If the fabric does not fray, I usually go with 0.6 cm, as I’m always short of fabric & it’s more convenient for sewing curves, but if you are not that confident in sewing yet, you might find 0.6 cm a bit challenging to work with.

So, let’s pretend, I decided to go with 1.2 cm seam allowance for both the collar & the neckline. In my case I will cut the rectangle of the following size (cm):

(L + 1.2 + 1.2) x (W × 2 + 1.2 + 1.2)

You don’t need to draw both stitching & cutting lines—remember what seam allowances you choose & go with cutting lines only.

Sewing

1. Make the placket

There are many types of button plackets & their patterns may vary, but for an easier explanation, I will use a simple single-fold packet as a reference in this post. Later you can adjust & apply this method to other types of packets.

In this example, the right front packet will be 2.5 cm wide when finished and the left one will be only 2 cm. I choose different widths to make sure that the stitching of the left packet won’t be visible when the blouse is buttoned up.

At first, apply fusible interfacing to the wrong side of the packet. Then, fold the vertical seam allowance to the wrong side, press & baste in place if necesary.

Now we will finish a small part of the neckline, which remains free from the collar so that you could easily tie the bow. On the wrong side, mark the fold of your placket with the basting thread or a chalk line. Fold the placket to the right side & baste the top part as shown.

On the neckline seam allowance draw a vertical line 1.5 cm* away from the placket fold. Draw the small part of the neck seamline between this mark & the fold. Now you have two chalk lines that form the letter “L”.

Stitch on these lines & make neat backstitches at the corner. This will prevent the fabric from fraying & tearing. You could use smaller stitches instead.

Cut a neat notch at the corner as shown. Trim seam allowances to 0.6 cm. Remove basting & turn the placket right side out, making sure you have nice corners & press.

*The idea is to leave 0.5 cm of the placket outside of this “L”. Since my smaller placket is 2 cm wide I will stitch only 1.5 of it. If you choose a wider one of, let’s say 3 cm, you will need to stitch 2.5 cm & so on.

Baste the placket in place & topstitch according to your design. You can topstitch both inner & outer edges.

2. Attach the collar

At this point, the shoulder seams of your blouse should be sewn & pressed.

Make thread marks at the CB lines of your collar piece & the blouse back piece. Place the collar & the blouse right sides together & match the CB marks. Baste or pin along the neckline.

Starting right at the junction of the placket & the neckline seam allowance, stitch the collar to the blouse. Finish exactly at the opposite placket junction.

Trim grade the blouse seam allowance to 0.6 cm. If necessary, neatly clip both seam allowances to release tension. Don’t cut too close to the stitching line to avoid fraying & tearing. Press seam allowances up to the collar.

At the opposite side of the collar—I will refer to it as the “facing”—thread trace the stitching line—the length of the basting line should be equal to the neckline seam. Later it’ll be easier to attach the collar facing to the neckline, using this basting as a guideline.

3. Sew the ties

Fold the collar right sides together. Make sure both sides are equal & baste the collar ties on the short sides. Baste the long sides & stop right at the the beginning of the stitching you made to join the collar & the blouse together.

At the front, temporarily turn nekline seam allowances down. Starting at the short sides, stitch both collar ties as shown. As with the basting, it is important to stop stitching exactly at the beginning of the stitching you made to join the collar & the blouse together. Be careful not to catch the blouse inside the stitching.

Remove basting at the ties & check that everything is neat. Trim grade the seam allowance of the ties' facing to 0.6 cm. If you have vertical short sides, notch or fold the seam allowances at the corvers, as shown. Folding the fabric would prevent fraying at the corners, but it mught be tricky to turn the ties right side out. If you have diagonal short sides, carefully notch the corners.

Using a seam roll press the long side seam allowances to the inner side of the tie. Turn the ties right side out. Make sure the corners look nice & press them.

4. Finish the collar

Using the basting you made before as a guideline turn in & press the seam allowance of the collar facing. Baste the fold in place so it goes right along the neckline seam. With neat tiny flip stitches attach this fold to the neckline. If making hand stitches is a bit hard to do, don’t worry just skip this.

Press the collar. Baste at the short & long bottom sides. Topstitch 0.2 cm on these sides. Hide all treads & press.

Now the bow collar is finished. Yay!

Enjoy your chic blouse.
